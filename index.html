What Happens After We Get an Offer

Once an offer comes in, we'll typically negotiate a counterproposal together, adjusting price, dates, and a few terms before both sides sign. From the moment we're both signed, closing generally happens in about 30 days. Here's how that window tends to unfold.

The Big Picture

Phase 1
Due Diligence & Inspection
Disclosures, title work, inspection, and insurance shopping. Roughly the first 1–2 weeks.
Phase 2
Appraisal & Loan
Lender orders the appraisal and finalizes loan approval. Overlaps with Phase 1 and runs into week 3.
Phase 3
Closing
All deadlines cleared, repairs completed, we sign and close. Typically around day 30.

Phase 1: Due Diligence & Inspection

SellerYou'll fill out a Seller's Property Disclosure and provide any documentation on past repairs or known issues with the unit. For the HOA documents, our preference is to let the title company gather them (about $125). That shifts both the effort and the liability for making sure nothing is missing onto the title company and the HOA, rather than resting on you.
Title CompanyPulls the HOA/association documents, works to clear any liens or encumbrances ahead of closing, and issues the title insurance policy on your behalf.
BuyerSchedules a professional inspection and can request repairs based on what's found. Given this is a newer condo with recently updated HVAC and kitchen appliances, we'd expect any requests to be fairly minor. The buyer will also shop for a homeowner's insurance quote during this window to confirm coverage is available and reasonably priced.

Phase 2: Appraisal & Loan

LenderOrders a third-party appraisal to confirm the home's value supports the loan amount. At the price we're discussing, we wouldn't expect any issues here.
LenderFinalizes underwriting and loan approval, evaluating both the buyer's qualifications and the property itself.

Phase 3: Closing

Once every deadline has passed or been resolved and any agreed-upon repairs are complete, we move to closing. You'll sign the closing documents, hand over keys and access items, and the sale is final.

Stone Canyon is approved for VA loans. It is not on the FHA-approved complex list, but an FHA spot approval is available, which carries roughly the same qualifications as a conventional loan, so it shouldn't meaningfully narrow the buyer pool.

Two Terms Worth Knowing

Almost everything in the contract timeline comes down to one of these two types of deadlines:

Termination Deadline

This is the buyer's window to simply walk away and get their earnest money back, no negotiation required. If they don't act by this date, that right expires.

Objection Deadline

This is the buyer's window to ask for a change or repair. We then need a signed resolution agreement from both sides by the resolution deadline, or the contract automatically terminates and earnest money goes back to the buyer.

Once We're Under Contract

This is the most important thing to understand about the process: the buyer has several built-in opportunities to terminate the contract along the way (inspection, appraisal, financing, title, and more). You, as the seller, generally do not have that same flexibility once we're signed. You can decline to make repairs or offer concessions, but if the buyer still wants to move forward, they have the right to do so. Because of that, it's worth being genuinely ready to sell before we countersign anything.

A Few Other Things to Have on Your Radar

  • Earnest money. Held in trust by the title company on behalf of both parties. It's returned to the buyer if they terminate properly under a deadline; it can become yours if the buyer defaults without a valid reason.
  • The final walk-through. The buyer has the right to walk through the property shortly before closing to confirm the condition hasn't changed and that any agreed repairs were completed.
  • Closing costs. As the seller, expect to cover items like the HOA status letter fee, any record change fee, prorated HOA dues, and your side of closing/title fees, in addition to commission.
  • Possession and keys. We'll confirm a possession date and time in the contract. At closing, you'll need to provide keys, garage remotes, and any access codes.
  • Utilities. Plan to keep utilities on through closing and coordinate the transfer or final readings once the sale is complete.
  • Marketing status. Once we're under contract, the listing typically moves to "pending" and active showings stop, so it's worth having your timeline (movers, next home, etc.) roughly mapped out.

Quick Deadline Glossary

DeadlineWhat It Means
Inspection ObjectionBuyer's window to request repairs based on the inspection findings.
Inspection ResolutionDeadline for both sides to sign off on any agreed repairs, or the contract terminates.
Appraisal DeadlineDate by which the buyer's appraisal must be completed.
Appraisal ObjectionBuyer's window to raise concerns if the appraised value comes in under the purchase price.
Title ObjectionBuyer's window to flag any title issues uncovered during the title search.
Loan/Financing DeadlinesA series of dates confirming the buyer's loan application, terms, and final approval are on track.
Closing DateThe day ownership officially transfers and funds are disbursed.
